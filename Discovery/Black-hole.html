black hole also known as a black hole or black hole, is a region of space-time that has such a strong gravitational field that no matter in general occupies certain mass and space or what radiation and light can. escape. General relativity predicts that a sufficiently large amount of matter in a small enough range will deform spacetime to become a black hole. Around a black hole is a surface defined by a mathematical equation called the event horizon, where matter passes through it unable to escape the black hole. A black hole is called "black" because it absorbs all radiation and matter that is attracted through the event horizon, like an absolute black body in thermodynamics; It is also not some kind of "hole" or "hole" but a region of spacetime that does not allow anything to escape.

Quantum field theory in curved spacetime predicts that a black hole's event horizon will emit radiation like a black body with a certain temperature emits thermal radiation. This temperature is inversely proportional to the mass of the black hole, making it difficult to observe this radiation for black holes of average or stellar mass.

In the 18th century, John Michell and Pierre-Simon Laplace considered an object with a strong gravitational field described by classical mechanics that made light impossible to escape. The first modern theory of the character of a black hole was given by Karl Schwarzschild in 1916 when he found the first exact solution to the Einstein field equation, [9] despite the physical significance and explanation of the spacetime region. the space in which nothing can escape was first invented by David Finkelstein in 1958.

For a long time physicists considered Schwarzschild's solution to be a pure mathematical description. Until the 1960s, new theoretical studies showed that black holes formed according to the strict predictions of general relativity. When astronomers discover neutron stars, pulsars and Cygnus X-1 - a black hole in a binary star system, predictions of a gravitational collapse become a reality, and the concept of black holes together with special bodies transformed into theories that describe these special entities in the universe.

In theory, stellar mass black holes form from the gravitational collapse of very large stars in the late stages of evolution. Once formed, they continue to attract matter from their surroundings, and their mass increases over time. Along with the process of merging and merging two or more black holes, there exist giant black holes with masses from several million to tens of billions of solar masses. Survey projects show that most at the center of a large galaxy exists at least one giant black hole.

Although by definition it is a completely or invisible black body, the existence of a black hole can be speculated through its interaction with its surroundings and radiation such as light. Matter falling into the black hole forms an accretion zone, where the matter collides with each other, becoming a plasma state that emits intense radiation; making the environment surrounding a black hole one of the brightest objects in the universe.

If there is a star orbiting the black hole, its shape and orbital period allows astronomers to calculate the black hole's mass and distance to it. These data help them distinguish whether a solid object is a black hole or a neutron star ... In this way, many of the detected black holes are in the binary star system, and at the center of the Milky Way there is a giant black hole. with a mass of approximately 4.3 million solar masses.

Properties and structure

T

he Hairless Theorem states that, once a stable condition is reached after formation, a stationary black hole only needs three independent physical parameters to describe it: mass, charge, and dynamic moment. Any two black holes that share these physical properties, or each pair of three equal parameters, cannot be distinguished from each other according to classical (or non-quantum) mechanics.

These properties are special because a person standing outside the black hole will measure these three parameters. For example, a charged black hole will repel other black holes with the same charge as in classical electrostatics. Similarly, the sum of mass (in the sense of energy + mass), the mass of ADM, inside a black hole can be found by using Gauss's law of gravity or observing the orbits of objects orbiting it. And for the moment of momentum, a distant person can be determined through the drag effect of the frame of reference caused by its rotation (the magnetic field of gravity).

When an object falls into a black hole, any information about the object's shape, charge distribution ... completely disappears for observers standing far away from the black hole. The property of the event horizon in this situation is like a dissipation system similar to a two-dimensional membrane, on which to visualize the existence of a frictional liquid that is charged and conducting, in four dimensional spacetime — or membrane model of black holes.

This property differs from other field theories of physics such as classical electromagnetic fields, in that they have no microscopic friction or conductivity, because they obey temporal symmetry, whereas a body Falling into a black hole cannot fly back out. Because the steady state after black hole formation only needs to be described by three parameters, there is no way to avoid losing information about the initial conditions: the gravitational and electromagnetic fields of the black hole give very little.

Primitive black holes after the Big Bang

G

ravitational collapse requires large matter density. In the current era of the universe these high densities existed only in stars, but at the time when the universe first formed after the Big Bang the matter density was very high at the time, with the ability to allow holes to form. primitive black. High density alone is not enough to allow a black hole to form because the uniform mass distribution does not cause matter to accumulate together.

For primitive black holes to form in this dense environment, there must initially be a fluctuation in density that allows matter to accumulate together through gravity. Different models of the early universe give different predictions about this degree of fluctuation. Some models predict that primitive black holes will form, ranging from Planck mass levels to hundreds of solar masses. Primitive black holes could be involved in the formation of medium mass black holes. space and super mass black holes.
